How to use this guide
📋

Each tab = one step. Open a tab to see what the step does, why it matters, what it produces, and the exact prompt to use.

📝

Copy the prompt. Each step has a ready-to-use prompt you can paste into any AI assistant — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or others.

Step 1 of 10
Discovery Questions
Turn your idea into a structured set of 20–35 sharp discovery questions
Why this step exists

The biggest mistake in product development is building before understanding.

Most people with an idea want to start building immediately. That's a trap. Without first interrogating the idea from every angle — who it's for, what pain it solves, how it makes money, who the competitors are, what the risks are — you end up building the wrong thing. These questions force clarity before a single dollar is spent or a line of code is written. Think of this as the diagnostic before the prescription.

What it produces
❓

Problem & Pain Questions

Who hurts? How much? What are they doing today without your product?

👥

Target Customer Questions

Who exactly is the buyer, the user, and the decision-maker?

💰

Business Model Questions

How does this make money? What's the revenue potential per customer?

⚔️

Competition & Market Questions

Who are the real competitors? What's the market size? Growing or shrinking?

🛠️

Product Shape Questions

Web app? Mobile? API? What are the 3–5 must-have features for the MVP?

⚠️

Risks & Constraints Questions

What's the biggest technical risk? What would make this product fail?

Output file
📄 01_discovery_questions.md
20–35 questions organized by category
Prompt to use
Paste this into any AI assistant
I have a product idea and I need you to help me think through it rigorously before I start building anything. MY IDEA: [Describe your product idea in 2–4 sentences. What does it do? Who is it for? What problem does it solve?] YOUR TASK: Generate 20–35 sharp, specific discovery questions that will help me fully understand what this product truly needs to be. Organize the questions into these categories: 1. Problem & Pain — Who hurts? How much? What workarounds exist today? 2. Target Customer — Who is the user? Who is the buyer? How many of them exist? 3. Value Proposition — What core value does this deliver? Why would someone switch to it? 4. Business Model — How does it make money? What are the cost drivers? 5. Competition & Market — Who are the real alternatives? Is the market growing? 6. Product Shape & Features — Web, mobile, or API? What are the 3–5 MVP must-haves? 7. Data & Integrations — What data does it need? What systems must it connect to? 8. Go-to-Market — How do you get the first 100 customers? 9. Success Metrics — How do you define success in 6 months? In 2 years? 10. Risks & Constraints — What's the biggest technical risk? What would cause this to fail? IMPORTANT: Make every question specific to MY idea — not generic MBA filler. Each question should be answerable and action-oriented. Show that you understood what I described. End with a note telling me what to do next once I've answered or thought through these questions.
Tips for best results
💡

Be specific in your idea description. The more concrete you are ("an app for freelance graphic designers to track invoice payments"), the more useful the questions will be. Vague input ("a productivity app") produces generic questions.

📝

Don't answer the questions yet. Just read through them. Some will reveal assumptions you didn't know you were making. That's the point of this step — to expose blind spots.

🚩

Flag the scary ones. The questions that make you uncomfortable or that you can't answer are the most important ones to research in Step 2.

Step 2 of 10
Market Research
Answer the discovery questions with real data, not gut feelings
🔗
Requires Step 1 output. Take the questions from Step 1 and paste them into this prompt. The AI will research each one using web search and domain knowledge.
Why this step exists

Most product ideas live and die on assumptions.

You have questions — now you need answers backed by evidence. This step replaces "I think the market is big" with "the TAM is $2.4B growing at 18% YoY (Statista 2024)." It replaces "there might be competitors" with "three specific competitors with names, pricing, and G2 reviews." Grounded research is the foundation of every good product decision. It also often reveals a fatal flaw early — before you've spent months building.

What it produces
📊

Market Size Data

Real TAM/SAM numbers from analyst reports or industry sources.

🏢

Competitor Analysis

Named competitors with pricing, reviews, strengths, and weaknesses.

📋

Regulatory & Compliance Context

Any legal or compliance risks that would affect the product.

⚠️

Key Uncertainties

Questions where data was thin — signals of where to do more validation.

Output file
📄 02_research_report.md
Full Q&A research document with sources and key uncertainties
Prompt to use
Paste this into any AI assistant
I have a set of product discovery questions about my idea. I need you to research and answer each one with real data, not opinions. MY PRODUCT IDEA: [Paste your idea description here] DISCOVERY QUESTIONS TO RESEARCH: [Paste all the questions from Step 1 here] YOUR TASK: Research and answer each question. Produce a structured report in this format: # Research Report: [Product Name] Date: [today's date] ## Summary 2–3 sentences on the key findings and what they mean for the product. ## Findings by Category [For each question category, answer every question with:] - Specific data points (market sizes, percentages, numbers) — not vague statements - Real company names for competitors — not hypothetical players - Source citations inline when pulling from research: "(Source: Statista, 2024)" - Clear language when uncertain: "Data is limited here, but based on similar markets..." ## Key Uncertainties List the questions where data was thin or contradictory, and what would resolve them. ## Recommended Next Steps 2–3 bullets on what to validate before building. QUALITY STANDARDS: - Prefer specific data: "$2.4B market growing at 18% YoY" beats "the market is large" - For competitors: name real products with real pricing pages and user reviews - If you find a fatal flaw in the idea, say it directly - Flag surprising findings — these are often the most valuable insights End by telling me to "refine this into a spec" as the next step.
Tips for best results
🌐

Use an AI with web search. This step benefits most from an AI that can browse the internet (Perplexity, Claude with search, ChatGPT with Browse). Without real-time search, the AI is working from training data which may be outdated for market size and competitor information.

🚩

Pay close attention to "Key Uncertainties." If the AI can't find data for a core assumption, that's a signal you need primary research — talking to potential customers or industry experts — before proceeding.

⭐

Surprising findings are gold. If the AI discovers a competitor with a 2.1-star rating on G2, or that a regulation just changed, or that the market is actually contracting — highlight those in your notes. These are the insights that shape strategy.

Step 3 of 10
Product Specification
Transform research into a complete, actionable product document
🔗
Requires Steps 1 + 2 output. Bring the discovery questions AND the research report into this prompt. The spec is built from both.
Why this step exists

A vague idea never gets built well.

The spec is the north star for every build decision. It aligns the team, prevents scope creep, and forces you to answer the hard questions before they become expensive problems mid-build. A good spec tells you exactly what the MVP is and what it is not, who the users are, what the tech stack should be, and how success will be measured. It's written to be useful — not bureaucratic. Engineers, designers, investors, and non-technical co-founders should all be able to read it and understand exactly what you're building.

What it produces — 12 sections
📝

Executive Summary + Problem Statement

One-paragraph pitch and a clear articulation of who hurts and why now.

🎯

Target Users + Value Proposition

Named personas, buyer vs. user distinction, and the core value delivered.

📦

Features & Scope (MVP, Phase 2, Future)

Specific features for each phase — not vague descriptions but concrete capabilities.

⚙️

Technical Specification + Data Model

Recommended tech stack with rationale and key data entities.

💼

Business Model + Go-to-Market

Revenue model, pricing structure, launch strategy, and sales motion.

📈

Success Metrics + Risks

KPI targets at 6 and 12 months, plus key risks and assumptions to validate.

Output file
📄 03_product_spec.md
Full 12-section product specification document
Prompt to use
Paste this into any AI assistant
I've done discovery and research on my product idea. Now I need you to turn this into a complete product specification document. DISCOVERY QUESTIONS: [Paste the questions from Step 1 here] RESEARCH REPORT: [Paste the full research report from Step 2 here] YOUR TASK: Produce a complete product specification document with these 12 sections: 1. Executive Summary — One compelling paragraph. Pitch-quality. 2. Problem Statement — The pain, who feels it, current workarounds, why now. 3. Target Users — Primary and secondary personas with enough detail to be real. 4. Value Proposition — Core value, 3–5 specific benefits, key differentiators. 5. Features & Scope — MVP (must-have), Phase 2 (should-have), Future (nice-to-have). Be specific: not "user dashboard" but "dashboard showing X, Y, Z with ability to filter by date and category." 6. User Flows — Step-by-step walk-through of the 2–3 most important user journeys. 7. Technical Specification — Architecture overview, recommended tech stack with rationale, high-level data model, non-functional requirements. 8. Business Model — Revenue model, pricing structure, key cost drivers, unit economics target. 9. Go-to-Market — Launch strategy, sales motion, key channels, launch pricing. 10. Success Metrics — Table of KPIs with 6-month and 12-month targets. 11. Risks & Assumptions — Technical risks, market risks, execution risks, and a list of assumptions that need validation. 12. Open Questions — Anything unresolved that needs a decision before building begins. WRITING RULES: - Be specific, not vague - Flag every assumption with [ASSUMPTION] so I know what needs validation - If inputs are incomplete, fill in reasonable defaults and flag them - Use tables for comparisons, bullets for lists, prose for narrative End by suggesting next steps (create a product story, or create an architecture diagram).
Tips for best results
📌

The spec is the master document. Steps 4, 6, 7, 8, and 9 all use the spec as their primary input. The better your spec, the better every downstream output will be. Spend time reviewing it before moving on.

✏️

Edit the [ASSUMPTION] flags. The AI will mark assumptions it's made. Go through them and either confirm, correct, or leave as open questions. This is where your domain knowledge beats the AI.

🎯

Be brutal about MVP scope. The MVP section is the most important. Push back if it seems too large — an MVP should take 2–3 months, not 18. Keep moving features to Phase 2 until only the absolute core remains.

Step 4 of 10
Product Story
Write a human narrative that makes everyone understand why this product matters
🔗
Requires Step 3 output. The product spec is the input. The story brings it to life with a real character, real pain, and real transformation.
Why this step exists

Data and specs convince engineers. Stories convince everyone else.

You'll need to communicate your product to investors, customers, co-founders, and non-technical partners. A feature list doesn't do that. A story does. The Story Mountain structure — used in film, advertising, and great product pitches — follows a real person from their daily struggle, through the breaking point, to discovering a solution, and finally to a transformed life. When someone reads it, they should think: "I know that person. I've felt that pain. I want that outcome."

What it produces — Story Mountain structure
🌅

Act 1: Exposition

Introduce a named, specific protagonist in their normal world. Make them feel real.

📈

Act 2: Rising Action

The problem emerges in a concrete, specific moment. Show the painful workaround breaking down.

🏔️

Act 3: Climax

Everything is on the line. A deadline, a client meeting, a financial hit. Something must change.

📉

Act 4: Falling Action

The protagonist uses the product. Show specific details — what they see, how it feels different.

🌄

Act 5: Resolution

3–6 months later: the new normal. Measurable outcomes. Life genuinely changed.

💬

Takeaway Line

One sentence capturing the transformation — suitable for a slide headline or tagline.

Output file
📄 04_product_story.md
600–900 word Story Mountain narrative
Prompt to use
Paste this into any AI assistant
I have a product specification and I need you to write a compelling narrative story about a real user whose life is transformed by this product. PRODUCT SPECIFICATION: [Paste your product spec from Step 3 here] YOUR TASK: Write a 600–900 word story using the Story Mountain structure — 5 acts following one specific protagonist from their daily pain to transformation: Act 1 — EXPOSITION: Introduce the protagonist by name. Give them a real role, company type, and daily context. Hint at the tension without making it a crisis yet. Make them feel like a specific real person, not a generic "user." Act 2 — RISING ACTION: The problem hits concretely. A specific moment — a deadline missed, a frustrated client email, a spreadsheet that crashed. Show what they're doing today as a workaround and why it's failing. Build tension. Act 3 — CLIMAX: Everything is on the line. A client meeting, a financial loss, an embarrassing failure. The reader should think "something has to change." The product enters the picture naturally here — not as a sales pitch. Act 4 — FALLING ACTION: Show the protagonist using the product specifically. What do they see? What do they do? How does it feel different? Show the friction disappearing. Show others noticing. Act 5 — RESOLUTION: 3–6 months later. What's different about their daily life? Show measurable outcomes — time saved, money made, stress reduced. End on a moment that makes the reader think "I want that." STYLE RULES: - Write in third-person, past tense - Use specific, concrete details — real tool names (Slack, Excel, QuickBooks), real numbers, real company sizes - Show don't tell: not "it was frustrating" — show the moment of frustration - Include 1–2 supporting characters (colleague, client, family member) who reflect the change - No product jargon — write for a smart non-technical friend - The product name appears naturally, not as a brand push End with a single "Takeaway" line capturing the transformation — suitable for a slide headline. Then tell me to "visualize this story as HTML panels" as the next step.
Tips for best results
👤

The protagonist is everything. Ask the AI to make the character as specific as possible — a name, an age, a city, a company type. "Sarah, a 34-year-old freelance graphic designer in Austin who bills through PayPal" is infinitely more compelling than "a typical freelancer."

🎭

The climax should hurt. The breaking point needs to feel real — a lost client, a missed payroll, a 2am crisis. If the story doesn't make you feel the pain, it won't land with an audience. Ask the AI to intensify the climax if it feels too gentle.

📊

The resolution needs numbers. "She saved 8 hours a week" or "revenue grew 40%" is far more powerful than "she felt better about her work." Make the AI anchor the outcome in specific, measurable improvement.

Step 5 of 10
Visual Storyboard
Turn the narrative into a 6-panel illustrated HTML presentation
🔗
Requires Step 4 output. The product story becomes the script for 6 illustrated panels. The AI translates each act of the story into a visual scene.
Why this step exists

A wall of text doesn't sell a product in a meeting. Six vivid panels do.

When you're presenting to investors, clients, or a board, you need something visual. This step creates a self-contained HTML file — open it in any browser — with six illustrated story panels. Each panel has an SVG illustration, a title, and a 2–3 sentence caption. It's the thing you put on the projector. You can email it as a single file. Print it as a handout. Share it as a link. No design skills required.

What it produces — 6 panels
😫

Panel 1: The Problem

"Chaos" — scattered documents, overwhelm, a stressed figure. Show the pain clearly.

⚡

Panel 2: The Trigger

"Entry Point" — the specific moment everything breaks. Clock, warning, crashing system.

🚪

Panel 3: Discovery

"First Contact" — user finds the product. A clean portal appearing. A hand reaching forward.

⚙️

Panel 4: How It Works

"The Engine" — data flowing through a funnel. What the product does, simplified visually.

🔍

Panel 5: The Shift

"The Insight" — dashboard with key data, magnifying glass, user gaining control and clarity.

🏆

Panel 6: The Outcome

"Before vs. After" — upward chart, relaxed figure, checkmarks. The measurable transformation.

Tips for best results
🎨

Specify your domain's color palette. Healthcare products should use calm blues and greens. Fintech should use sophisticated navy and gold. Consumer apps can be vibrant. Tell the AI the domain and it will pick appropriate colors automatically.

🖨️

Print-ready is important. The HTML file should include print styles so it renders cleanly as a one-page handout. If presenting in a meeting, you may want to print it and leave copies with attendees.

Step 6 of 10
Architecture Diagram
Visualize how all the technical components fit together
🔗
Requires Step 3 output. The product spec drives the architecture — tech stack, integrations, data flows, and infrastructure are all defined there.
Why this step exists

Architecture diagrams are the shared language between engineers, PMs, and executives.

Without a diagram, everyone has a different mental model of how the system works. Engineers argue about where things should live. PMs make promises that break the architecture. Executives don't understand why something is "technically complex." A clear architecture diagram fixes all of this. It also surfaces missing components — integrations you forgot about, security layers you didn't design, or services that create a single point of failure. Better to find those gaps on paper than in production.

What it produces
👥

Client / User Layer

Web browser, mobile app, API consumers — who interacts with the system.

🖥️

Frontend Layer

The UI framework, routing, and presentation components.

⚙️

Backend & Services Layer

API gateway, auth service, core business logic modules.

🗄️

Data Layer

Primary database, cache, file storage, and data flows between them.

🔌

External Integrations

Third-party APIs: Stripe, Auth0, SendGrid, analytics tools, and others.

🔍

Interactive Tooltips

Hover over any component to see a description of what it does.

Output file

Interactive SVG architecture diagram — hover tooltips, color-coded layers
Prompt to use
Paste this into any AI assistant
I need you to create a professional interactive architecture diagram for my product as a self-contained HTML file. PRODUCT SPECIFICATION (with tech stack): [Paste your product spec from Step 3 here — especially the Technical Specification section] YOUR TASK: Generate a single self-contained HTML file (arch_diagram.html) with an interactive SVG architecture diagram. The diagram must show: 1. All major system components from the spec 2. All external integrations and APIs 3. Authentication and authorization flow 4. Data storage (databases, caches, file storage) 5. Key data flows between components (with directional arrows and labels) 6. Tips for best results
🔍

Look for gaps in the diagram. Missing an authentication layer? No logging/monitoring service? No CDN? The diagram makes missing infrastructure visible at a glance. These gaps are easier to address in planning than in production.

👥

Share this with technical co-founders or engineers early. Engineers often have strong opinions about architecture. Sharing this diagram early — before anyone writes code — is the cheapest time to have those debates and course-correct.

Step 7 of 10
Team & Staffing Plan
Define exactly who you need to build and run this product
🔗
Requires Step 3 output. The product spec defines the product complexity, which determines the team size, skill mix, and costs needed.
Why this step exists

Products fail not because of bad ideas but because of wrong teams.

Too small a team and you miss deadlines. Wrong skill mix and you build something nobody can maintain. Right people at the wrong phase and you burn cash before product-market fit. The team plan makes the human capital requirements concrete and plannable — not "we'll need some engineers" but "we need one Senior Backend Engineer at $160K and one Mid Full-Stack at $130K in Month 1, then a Product Designer in Month 3." This precision is what separates fundable plans from hopeful guesses.

What it produces
🚀

Phase 1: MVP Team (0–6 months)

Minimum credible team to ship. Every role with seniority, cost, and responsibilities.

📈

Phase 2: Growth Team (6–18 months)

Roles added as the product gains traction. Running headcount and burn rate.

🏢

Phase 3: Scale Structure (18+ months)

Full org structure with reporting hierarchy for a mature product.

💰

Cost Summary by Phase

Monthly burn rate and total phase cost for financial planning.

🎯

Hiring Priority Order

Who to hire first and exactly why — not alphabetically, but strategically.

🛒

Build vs. Buy vs. Outsource

What to hire for vs. use SaaS tools to avoid an early hire entirely.

Output file
📄 07_team_plan.md
Full staffing plan with roles, costs, and hiring sequence across 3 phases
Prompt to use
Paste this into any AI assistant
I need a detailed, realistic team staffing plan for my product. Tell me exactly who I need, when, and what it will cost. PRODUCT SPECIFICATION: [Paste your product spec from Step 3 here] CONTEXT: [Optional: add any constraints — bootstrap vs. funded, US vs. remote, specific budget ceiling] YOUR TASK: Produce a full team staffing plan covering three phases: Phase 1: MVP (Months 0–6) — minimum credible team to ship Phase 2: Growth (Months 6–18) — roles added as the product gains traction Phase 3: Scale (Month 18+) — full org for a mature product For each role in Phases 1 and 2, specify: - Role title (be specific: "Senior Full-Stack Engineer" not "Developer") - Headcount - Key responsibilities (3–4 bullets) - Required skills (specific technologies) - Seniority level (Junior / Mid / Senior / Staff) - Engagement type (Full-time / Contractor / Co-founder) - Estimated monthly cost in USD (salary range — add 25–30% for benefits/overhead) Format as: role table + detail section per role + total monthly burn. Also include: - Hiring Priority Order: who to hire first and why — ordered by strategic importance - Build vs. Buy vs. Outsource: which roles to avoid by using SaaS tools instead - Key Risks: single points of failure, hard-to-hire skills, underestimated roles - Total Cost Summary table: all phases side by side BE REALISTIC: Match the team size to the product complexity. A simple SaaS might need 3 people. A complex platform might need 12. Don't pad the team. Don't under-staff either.
Tips for best results
💡

"Build vs. Buy" is often the most valuable section. The AI will often identify roles you don't need to hire for because a $50/month SaaS tool can replace them (e.g., "use Auth0 instead of building auth — saves 2–3 weeks and is more secure").

🌍

Specify your hiring market. US salaries are very different from European or remote-first teams. If your team will be in Eastern Europe, Southeast Asia, or Latin America, the AI can benchmark appropriately if you mention it.

Step 8 of 10
Frontend Prototype
Build a clickable, polished HTML demo of your product's interface
🔗
Requires Steps 3 + 4 output. The spec defines the screens and features. The story provides the domain context and personas that inform the realistic mock data.
Why this step exists

You can't sell a product from a spec document.

A working prototype — even one with fake data — lets clients click around, feel the flow, and say "yes, that's what we want" (or "no, that's not right"). It collapses the feedback loop from weeks of design and development to minutes. It lets you validate assumptions with real users before writing a single line of production code. And it gives investors something tangible to react to in a meeting — far more powerful than a slide deck.

What it produces
🏠

Dashboard / Home Screen

The main hub with key metrics, charts, and primary navigation.

⚡

Core Feature Screen

The primary thing the product does — the screen users visit most.

🔍

Detail / Drill-Down View

What happens when you click into a specific item or record.

📋

Realistic Mock Data

Domain-appropriate data (real names, real numbers, real states) — no Lorem Ipsum.

🖱️

Working Navigation

Clicking menu items actually switches screens. Modals open and close.

📊

Charts & Data Visualization

At least one SVG chart showing the product's key metrics.

Why this step exists

Monolithic thinking kills product delivery.

When you try to build everything at once, nothing works until everything works — which means nothing works for a very long time. Decomposing the product into modules lets you: parallelize work across multiple engineers, set clear milestones with demonstrable progress, assign clear ownership so nobody is stepping on each other, and ship partial value early while the rest is still being built.
